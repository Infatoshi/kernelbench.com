What I built

The kernel fuses int4 unpack + AWQ-style dequant + GEMM in one pass, dispatched on M:

M = 1 (decode, bandwidth-bound) — custom CUDA GEMV via load_inline:

  • Splits K at packed-row granularity (finer than the 128-wide group) so it reaches full occupancy and keeps vectorized (uint32) coalesced loads — a tensor-core GEMM's group-level split-K is capped at 32 and can't fill 188 SMs for M=1.
  • Fully unrolls the 64-row inner loop — the single biggest win (766→1004 GB/s). Profiling showed the kernel is latency/MLP-bound, not raw-bandwidth-bound; full unroll floods the pipeline with independent loads. Reaches 71% of DRAM peak (1.2 TB/s).
  • Two variants (intra-block reduction = one launch/no memset; atomic split-K = more live blocks for small N).

M ≥ 16 (prefill) — Triton tensor-core GEMM with split-K, even/odd nibbles as two K-halves, bf16 dequant-then-dot.

Key findings (saved to memory)

  • The benchmark flushes L2 before each call, and Triton's autotuner times L2-hot → it picks the wrong configs. I swept every config with my own L2-cold timing and hardcoded per shape.
  • Returning the fp32 accumulator directly avoids a bf16-convert kernel (check compares in fp32; byte-count is fixed).
  • Numerics: the affine-correction factoring (x·w_int − z·Σx) catastrophically cancels under the ×64 large-activation stress (fails M=256 by 1 elem in 3.1M); the GEMM must use per-element bf16 dequant to match the reference's rounding.

Honest limits

The GEMM shapes (M=16/32/256) are the remaining bottleneck — Triton's tl.dot GEMM plateaus at ~22% occupancy (register-limited by the dequant tile). A naive nvcuda::wmma GEMM I wrote was worse than Triton; beating it needs a Marlin-style cp.async double-buffered mma kernel, which I judged too high-risk for the remaining budget. M=256 is compute-bound and structurally capped (~0.07–0.12) in the memory-regime metric. The path forward is documented in memory.
