What the kernel does

A real fp8×fp8 tensor-core MMA (fp8 inputs, fp32 accumulate) via Triton tl.dot, with the per-output-channel scale applied post-accumulation. Three key engineering decisions drove the number up from a naive 0.28:

  1. K-padding to 128. The fp8 MMA has native K=32; a tl.dot whose K-mask isn't a multiple of 32 (e.g. the K=4127 tail of 31) collapses off the tensor cores onto a SIMD path — 9× slower. I zero-pad K to a multiple of 128 (weight pre-padded into a persistent buffer; activation padded per-call and cached), so the reduction loop runs a clean, mask-free, fully-tensor-core pipeline. This alone took shape 1 from 0.23 → 0.66.
  2. Direct launch, no autotune. Autotune both picked worse configs and added ~10µs/call. I hardcode the tile config per shape (large-M → 128×256×128; skinny → 16×128×256) and precompute the grid and strides.
  3. CUDA graph capture. Triton's launch overhead (~5–13µs of arg marshalling) is a large fraction of the skinny kernel. I capture the kernel into a graph on first call and replay thereafter (the persistent wpad buffer keeps the graph valid across in-place weight mutations by the stress harness; capture falls back gracefully).

Why ~0.41 is the ceiling here

I verified this is the hardware limit for plain fp8 e4m3 on the RTX PRO 6000 (consumer Blackwell sm120), not a tuning shortfall:

  • ncu: compute shapes are tensor-pipeline-bound at 75% (1 CTA/SM, 99KB shared-mem cap, 64K regs); skinny hits 84.7% of peak DRAM (bandwidth-maxed, ~0.09 is its ceiling).
  • CUTLASS tcgen05 (I built and ran it via load_inline with the torchao sm120 collective, cluster 1×1×1) — it compiles and runs but tops out at 0.690, identical to Triton's mma.sync. Consumer-Blackwell's high-throughput path is block-scaled NVFP4, which doesn't apply to per-channel scaling. TMA descriptors were slower too. The forbidden cuBLAS path's 0.77 uses an inaccessible proprietary kernel.
